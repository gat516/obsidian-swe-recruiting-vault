recognition, generation effect, spaced retrieval, and durable schema-building (not "pretty notes"). > four parallel tracks: **technical** (sections 1–7), **behavioral** (section 8), **applications** applications, and resume defense. --- ## design philosophy (read this first) the vault is **pattern-first, problem-second**. problem notes are leaves; pattern hubs are the trunk. when you study, you should be moving *up* from a problem to a pattern hub, then *across* to other problems sharing that deep structure. that movement is the expert-categorization wiring you're trying to build. three rules that drive every design choice below: 1. **generation > consumption.** every template forces you to write something *before* you read anything. restatement, signal guess, and approach attempt come before any solution discussion. if a template has a section that lets you passively paste in a solution, it's wrong. 2. **pattern hubs are the asset.** a polished problem note is worth less than a half-finished pattern hub. the hub is where transfer happens. most of your re-reading should be hubs, not individual problems. 3. **excalidraw is for traces, not decoration.** use it where the *behavior* of an algorithm is hard to see in code: pointer movement, recursion trees, DP tables filling in, graph traversal frontiers. not for ER diagrams of your folder structure. these rules generalize to the other tracks: stories are leaves, question hubs are trunks. bullets are leaves, project deep-dives are trunks. every track has the same shape. --- ## folder structure ``` algorithm on a small input. pointer positions, hash map state, queue contents, recursion stack. five minutes of drawing is worth twenty of staring. **inside a pattern hub:** the *mental model* of the pattern. for sliding window, draw a window expanding/contracting on an array. for bfs, draw the level-by-level frontier. for dp, draw the table being filled with arrows showing dependencies. **inside a framework note:** a decision tree. **for data structures:** one drawing per structure showing the invariant. **for behavioral:** the "story flex map" — central nodes are stories, spokes are question categories. **for projects (★ new):** an architecture diagram per project. the test is: can you reproduce this on a whiteboard in 5 minutes cold? if no, the diagram is too complex (or you don't actually know the project as well as you think). re-draw weekly without referencing the existing one as a real drill. **workflow tip:** make a recurring drawing called `_scratchpad.excalidraw` that you wipe weekly. --- ## a typical study session (the technical workflow) 1. **open obsidian** → homepage opens automatically → see today's targets. 2. **pick the problem** from your study plan. open the problem note. 3. **time floor: 15–20 minutes solo.** fill in sections 1–4 (the ticket). no googling, no hints, no ai. 4. **if you solved it:** fill section 5, then section 6 (pattern / signal / why / gotcha). draw the trace. 5. **if you're stuck after 20 min:** *only now* engage your ai thinking partner with the ticket pasted in. once you solve, sections 5 and 6 still get filled — by you, not pasted from the ai. 6. **update the pattern hub.** new gotcha? add it. the hub is the asset. 7. **generate one flashcard.** 8. **daily log:** fill in outcome, hint level, time-to-approach.
